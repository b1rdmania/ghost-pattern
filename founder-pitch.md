# Ghost Pattern — Founder Pitch

---

Most tools ask you to build a beat.

This one asks you to understand one.

---

## The gap

Between sample packs and DAWs, there is nothing that teaches feel. Loop packs give you a product, not a lesson. DAWs give you infinite possibility and no point of view. Neither tells you why a Chicago house groove from 1991 feels the way it does, why the snare sits where it sits, or what would break if you "corrected" it.

That knowledge — groove knowledge — lives in producer communities, YouTube tutorials, and scattered blog posts. It is not in any tool. It is not interactive. And it is not connected to the act of making.

---

## The product

**Ghost Pattern** is a browser-first groove learning environment built around one object: the **Groove Study**.

A Groove Study is a curated encounter with one rhythmic idea from a scene and era. It contains a canonical pattern, two to four authored variations, a short annotation explaining what gives the groove its feel and what not to correct, and a groove profile describing its swing, accent logic, and microtiming tendencies.

When a user opens the app, they do not land on an empty grid. They land inside a study:

*"Detroit Techno — Industrial Kick and Hat Architecture, 1993"*  
*"NYC Garage — Gospel Swing Study, 1991"*  
*"UK Acid — Off-Grid Snare Study, 1992"*

The grid is there. It is editable. But it is in service of the study, not the other way around.

---

## Why this is not a sequencer

Sequencers are empty. They are neutral. Their value proposition is possibility.

Ghost Pattern is opinionated. Its value proposition is taste. A user who spends twenty minutes inside a Detroit techno study should leave with a feeling they did not have before — not just a MIDI file, but a sense of why the hat sits where it sits and what happens to the groove when you move it.

That requires curation, not features. The moat is editorial, not technical.

---

## Why mutation is the right mechanic

Users do not just listen. They diverge.

The mutation panel lets a user take the canonical pattern and adjust swing amount, ghost note density, hat variation, and accent position — staying inside the scene's rhythmic vocabulary. The kick never changes. The groove preset identity never changes. The result is always traceable back to the archetype it came from.

This is not a black box. The user hears exactly what they changed. That is the lesson.

---

## Why export is a by-product, not the pitch

MIDI export is there. It works. Patterns drop cleanly into Ableton, Logic, or FL Studio.

But export is not why someone opens this product. They open it because they want to understand something. Export is how that understanding becomes useful.

The difference matters for how you build, how you write copy, and how you measure success.

---

## Phase 1: what it feels like

The user lands inside a Groove Study. The pattern is armed at step 0. The annotation is visible — three sentences explaining the feel. They press play.

They hear something that sounds like it belongs to a place and time. They read the annotation. They press play again.

They adjust swing. They add a ghost note. They swap the kit. They export.

They share a URL that a friend opens and hears exactly the same thing.

That is Phase 1. Twelve to eighteen studies across four to six scenes. Three scene kits. A mutation panel. MIDI export. URL sharing.

No accounts. No social feed. No piano roll. No arrangement view.

---

## Why now, why here

The 1988–2008 house and techno canon is under-documented as interactive material. It is heavily documented as history. No one has made it playable and teachable in a browser.

Dig — the catalog this product connects to — provides the scene taxonomy backbone. That means the metadata is not built from scratch. The studies project onto an existing, curated graph.

The content is the hard part. The technology is not. The window is open because no one has done the editorial work.
